Theater review Met?s ?For Colored Girls? retains its relevance By ROBERT TRUSSELL The Kansas City Star Updated: 2013-03-04T03:50:53Z March 3 By ROBERT TRUSSELL The Kansas City Star What we think of as the 1960s ? freedom movements, protests, an explosion of creativity in the arts ? actually extended well into the 1970s before petering out about the time Jimmy Carter was elected. Order Reprints Kay Connelly Aishah Harvey plays the Lady in Yellow in "For Colored Girls" at Metropolitan Ensemble Theatre. Onstage ?For Colored Girls Who Have Considered Suicide When the Rainbow Is Enuf? runs through March 10 at Metropolitan Ensemble Theatre, 3614 Main St. For more information, call 816-569-3226 or go to METKC.org . More News * //Ron Megee sinks his teeth into a new work * //Strong performances, earthy humor lift uneven ?Good People? * //Surrealistic ?Fool for Love? comes to life at the Living Room * //Met?s ?For Colored Girls? retains its relevance * //Strong performances, earthy humor lift uneven 'Good People' Read more Robert Trussell The Metropolitan Ensemble Theatre is revisiting the era with a dramatic work that still packs a punch. Ntozake Shange?s 1976 play ?For Colored Girls Who Have Considered Suicide When the Rainbow Is Enuf? feels firmly rooted in its era, although the feminist issues Shange talks about are every bit as relevant now as they were a generation ago. Shange has updated the material to include a section on AIDS and has replaced Vietnam with Iraq/Afghanistan in reflecting the social cost of a protracted war. The changes are pretty seamless, although they don?t really do much to enhance the work?s relevance. Described as a ?choreopoem? by Shange, the extended one-act compresses about 20 verse pieces into its running time. There are no conventional characters and no conventional plot. The show is performed by seven actresses identified only by colors ? the Lady in Green, the Lady in Red, and so on ? who enunciate poetic passages that don?t directly relate to one another. There is thematic unity, however. One has to do with the bond of African-American women based on their collective experience. The other throws into sharp relief the exploitation and abuse of women by men. That?s not to say the play is a downer. Shange weaves quite a bit of honest humor into the script. She also pulls back at moments to allow us a sense of historical perspective. A passage describing a Creole carnival in Natchez, Miss., vividly captures a moment in time brought to life by Shange?s muscular and richly visual language. Director Karen Paisley has put together a gifted cast and stages the piece with choices that, for the most part, capture the power and meaning of Shange?s language. Shannon Smith-Regnier?s costumes are simple but vivid, and the show is enhanced inestimably by Greg Casparian?s lighting design. I?ve seen some but not all of these performers, who generally bring specificity and controlled emotional power to the show. Chioma Anyanwu, Victoria Barbee, Donette Coleman, Aishah Harvey, Lynn King, Sherri Roulette Mosley and Meredith Wolfe represent various ages and physical types, but their evident joy of working with one another other comes through clearly. Each performer has memorable moments, but Mosley as the Lady in Red creates indelible images with her emotional enactment of a passage late in the show about a mother dealing with the violent man in her life ? a war veteran and crack addict. The challenges faced by the women onstage are sometimes harsh, always difficult and depressingly eternal. Ultimately, however, the ladies of color find a bit of grace as they chant the refrain; ?I found God in myself and I love her fiercely.? It?s a moment of uplift that allows the play to conclude on a hopeful note. To reach Robert Trussell, call 816-234-4765 or send email to rtrussell@kcstar.com . Copyright 2013 The Kansas City Star.
